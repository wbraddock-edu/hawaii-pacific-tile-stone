Best Flooring for Hawaii's Humidity

📅 June 2025✍ Fernando Reyes⏱ 8 min read

Choosing flooring for a Hawaii home is genuinely different from choosing flooring anywhere else in the continental US. Hawaii's persistent humidity, temperature fluctuations, occasional heavy rain, and salt-air environment create conditions that accelerate the failure of materials that work perfectly in drier climates. Here's our comprehensive guide to what actually performs in Hawaii homes.

What the Humidity Does

Wood flooring absorbs and releases moisture seasonally, causing expansion and contraction. In Hawaii's consistently high humidity, wood is in a near-constant state of moisture absorption. This leads to cupping, warping, and separation at the seams — problems that appear within years, not decades, in homes that see the humidity swings common on Kauai's North Shore or windward Oahu.

Porcelain Tile: The Top Performer

High-density porcelain tile is our top recommendation for Hawaiian floors. It's dimensionally stable — it doesn't expand or contract with humidity changes. It's impervious to water, resistant to mold and mildew, doesn't harbor allergens, and cleans beautifully. With today's large-format tiles and realistic stone-look finishes, porcelain achieves the aesthetics of natural stone or wood with dramatically better performance.

Natural Stone: Excellent with Proper Care

Natural stone — travertine, slate, basalt, and marble — performs well in Hawaii when properly sealed and maintained. The key is consistent annual sealing. Unsealed natural stone in Hawaii's humidity will eventually absorb moisture and potentially support mold growth in the stone or grout. Sealed properly, natural stone can be stunning and last generations.

What to Avoid

Solid hardwood flooring in high-humidity areas of Hawaii is generally a poor choice unless the home has robust, consistent climate control. Laminate flooring — which uses a wood-composite core — is similarly vulnerable. Carpet traps humidity and is a mold risk in Hawaii homes without consistent air conditioning. Vinyl plank flooring performs better than wood but its lower end to subfloor glue can fail in sustained moisture.
